About This Instrument

The Operational Fault & Alignment Analysis™ is a self-administered executive diagnostic instrument. It is designed for founders and CEOs of scaling organisations who sense that something in their business is structurally misaligned — but have not yet been able to name it precisely.

It is not a business book. It is not a workbook or a template. It is a diagnostic instrument: clinical, precise, and built on the same Structural Intelligence methodology that underpins the Operational Intelligence Audit™.

The instrument guides you through a structured assessment across five structural fault categories — each one targeting a specific class of organisational dysfunction. By the end, you will have a Fault Profile: a named, scored diagnosis of where your organisation's structural weight is coming from and why you have been feeling it without being able to articulate it.

Answer based on how your organisation currently operates — not how you intend it to. Each statement is rated on a 1–5 scale from Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree.

Fault Category A · 1 of 5
Fault A

Authority Dissolution

Authority assigned but never embedded. Measures whether authority within the organisation exists structurally or only in name.

What this fault measures

Authority Dissolution occurs when an organisation has assigned leadership titles and responsibilities — but has never structurally transferred the actual power to act within those roles. Titles exist. Authority does not.

In scaling organisations, this fault develops because leadership positions are often created to manage growth, but the decision-making boundaries of those roles are never explicitly defined, communicated, or embedded. As a result, role holders default to seeking approval, and founders or senior leaders default to retaining control — even when neither party intends to.

How it typically surfaces
Leaders hold titles but consistently defer decisions upward
Approval loops exist where autonomous action should
Team members are unsure what they are permitted to decide without escalation
The organisation moves at the speed of the founder, not the speed of the structure
How to respond: Rate each statement based on how your organisation currently operates — not how it is intended to, or how it operated in an earlier stage. There are no correct answers. Accuracy produces the most useful diagnostic signal.

Fault Category B · 2 of 5
Fault B

Accountability Diffusion

Ownership distributed to everyone and therefore no one. Measures whether accountability is structurally embedded or informally assumed.

What this fault measures

Accountability Diffusion occurs when ownership of outcomes is distributed broadly across teams, groups, or shared roles — to the point where no single individual holds clear, personal accountability for a specific result. When everyone is responsible, no one is.

This fault is often well-intentioned. It emerges from collaborative cultures, flat structures, and a desire to share ownership. But structurally, diffused accountability creates a system where outcomes are not reliably delivered, consequences are softened, and performance gaps are obscured behind collective language.

How it typically surfaces
Post-mortems on missed outcomes produce no clear individual owner
KPIs and targets are assigned to teams rather than named individuals
Conversations about underperformance become conversations about process
Leadership cannot answer: "Who is personally accountable for this result?"
How to respond: Rate each statement based on your honest observation of how accountability actually functions — not how it is described in documentation or policy. Consider recent examples of outcomes, delivered or missed, as you respond.

Fault Category C · 3 of 5
Fault C

Founder Dependency Architecture

The invisible load-bearing wall built around you. Measures the degree to which the organisation's structural weight rests on the founder.

What this fault measures

Founder Dependency Architecture is the structural condition in which the organisation's operating capacity — its ability to make decisions, maintain relationships, resolve problems, and move forward — is architecturally dependent on the founder's active involvement. It is not a personality trait. It is a structural design flaw.

This fault typically develops not through poor intent but through the natural accumulation of a founder's centrality during early growth. The organisation builds itself around the founder's knowledge, relationships, and judgment — and never structurally transfers that load to the architecture itself.

How it typically surfaces
The founder's absence — even briefly — creates visible operational drag
Key client relationships, partnerships, and knowledge live in one person
The team functions at reduced capacity when the founder is unavailable
Strategic and operational momentum depends on the founder's active presence
How to respond: This section asks you to assess your organisation's structural dependency on you directly. Answer based on what is structurally true — not what you aspire to build. Honest responses here produce the most actionable diagnostic output.

Fault Category D · 4 of 5
Fault D

Decision Layer Compression

Your organisation has a queue, not a decision layer. Measures whether decisions are made at the appropriate level or compressed upward.

What this fault measures

Decision Layer Compression occurs when decisions that should be resolved at a lower organisational level are consistently escalated upward — compressing the decision-making load onto senior leadership or the founder. The organisation has people in leadership roles, but not a functioning decision layer.

This fault is often misread as a talent problem or a confidence problem. It is neither. It is a structural problem: decision rights have not been clearly assigned, the consequences of autonomous decision-making are unclear, and the organisation has implicitly trained its people that escalation is the correct default.

How it typically surfaces
Senior calendars are filled with decisions that should not require senior involvement
Organisational pace is governed by approval queues, not decision-making capacity
Leaders with authority still seek sign-off before acting
The same categories of decision recur at the top of the organisation repeatedly
How to respond: Consider the pattern of decisions reaching you or senior leadership over the past 30 to 90 days. Rate each statement based on that observable pattern — not on what the org chart implies should be happening.

Fault Category E · 5 of 5
Fault E

Structural Opacity

The company on paper versus the one that actually operates. Measures whether the organisation's real operating architecture is visible and legible.

What this fault measures

Structural Opacity occurs when the organisation that exists in documentation — the org chart, the job descriptions, the stated processes — does not reflect how the organisation actually operates. There is a formal structure and a real structure, and they are not the same.

In scaling organisations, this gap develops rapidly. Informal workarounds become embedded. Power concentrates in unexpected places. Critical knowledge lives in individuals rather than systems. The result is an organisation that is increasingly illegible — to its leaders, to new team members, and to itself.

How it typically surfaces
New hires take an extended period to understand "how things actually work"
There are informal decision pathways that bypass the stated structure
Critical processes depend on specific individuals, not documented systems
Leadership cannot fully map the organisation's real operating architecture
How to respond: This is the final fault category. Rate each statement based on the gap between your organisation's documented structure and its actual day-to-day operating reality. This section often surfaces the most significant latent risk in scaling organisations.
Please respond to all statements before continuing.
